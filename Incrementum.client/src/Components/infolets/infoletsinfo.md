## EPS
EPS stands for earnings per share. It is calculated by taking the net income of the company and money owed to preferred shareholders, for a given quarter/year, then dividing by average weighted outstanding shares. Typically a higher number means a better return for the investor
## D/E ratio
Debt to equity ratio. The amount of liability a company has divided by the shareholders equity. A lower number indicates stability while a higher number indicates volitility or insolvency.
## 52 week high
As the name suggests this metric is the highest value the stock got over the last 52 weeks. If this is higher the stock could have been overvalued or currently at a low point.
## 52 week low
The lowest stock price for the last 52 weeks could be used to indicate growth.
## 1 day % change
The amount in percentage format that the stock gained or lost red for lost green for gained. Used to see a stock price’s trajectory.

## Mkt. cap
Market capitalization is the total valuation of the outstanding shares. You can use it to tell how much you could make in a large scale trade
## Outstanding shares
The amount of shares held by the shareholder. It itself is a metric used in other metrics.
## Share class
Just different classifications of the shares. Class A is higher than Class B etc.
## Annual eps growth
The amount that a companies eps has changed in the last year.
## PE ratio 
Price to equity ratio. Price divided by EPS. The higher the ratio the higher the expected growth.
## PEG ratio
Price per Share / Earnings per Share) ÷ Expected Earnings Growth Rate.
## P/S Ratio
Price to sales. Price per stock divided by sales made by the company. Useful in identifying undervalued stocks.
